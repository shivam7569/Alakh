# Breathing technique catalog

Researched and adversarially safety-verified for the Alakh app (45 techniques). Generated into `core/.../rules/BreathingCatalog.kt`. **Not medical advice.**

> These breathing exercises are for general wellbeing and are not medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. If you have a health condition or are pregnant, check with a clinician first. Stop and breathe normally if you feel dizzy, lightheaded, tingling, faint, or short of breath. If symptoms persist or you have chest pain, seek medical help.

**Safety tiers:** `GENERAL` = fine for almost anyone · `CAUTION` = retention/extended holds some should avoid · `ADVANCED` = hyperventilation/strong retention with real contraindications. CAUTION and ADVANCED require a one-time acknowledgement before starting.

**Retention/hyperventilation warning:** This technique involves breath-holding or rapid (hyperventilation) breathing, which can cause sudden dizziness or fainting without warning. NEVER practice in or near water (bath, pool, or open water) or while driving or operating machinery. Always sit or lie down in a safe place, and stop immediately if you feel faint.

## FOCUS

### Mindful Breath Counting  `GENERAL`
*aka Counting the Breath, Susokukan (Zen breath counting), Count-to-Ten Breathing, Breath Counting Meditation*

- **Pattern:** FREEFORM (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Breathe naturally, nasal preferred; no forcing of rate or depth.
- Breathe naturally and silently count each exhale (up to 10, then restart) as an attention anchor. A meditation-style reset for focus, not a paced rhythm.
- *How it works:* No rhythm is imposed; the breath stays natural while counting gives the mind a single object of attention, training sustained focus and gently nudging breathing toward a calmer, more regular pattern.
- **Benefits:** Builds sustained attention and focus; Reduces rumination and mind-wandering; Calming without any breath manipulation; Portable and equipment-free
- **Safety:** Very low physical risk. If focusing on the breath causes dizziness or distress, stop and breathe normally.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Trauma/PTSD or active panic: interoceptive focus can surface intrusive memories — use an external anchor (sound, surroundings) and stop if distress rises
  - Strong health/body anxiety or eating disorders: breath focus can be activating; an external anchor is fine
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (attention narrows, often eyes closed)
- *Sources:* Zen/Buddhist tradition (Susokukan); MBSR (mindfulness-based stress reduction); Levinson et al., Frontiers in Psychology 2014

### Samavritti (Equal Breathing with Holds)  `CAUTION`
*aka Sama Vritti, Equal-Ratio Breathing, Box Breathing (4-part variant)*

- **Pattern:** in 4.0s / hold 4.0s / out 4.0s / hold 4.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Nasal breathing; equal counts for each phase.
- A balancing four-part square breath where each phase — inhale, hold, exhale, hold — is held for an equal count. The same family as box breathing, framed for calm-alert focus.
- *How it works:* Equal-length phases impose a steady, predictable rhythm that regulates the autonomic nervous system and supports HRV. The even cadence and counting anchor attention, balancing sympathetic and parasympathetic activity to produce calm alertness. The two short holds are deliberate breath retention.
- **Benefits:** Balances and steadies the nervous system; Improves focus and calm alertness; Reduces stress and anxiety; Good preparation for meditation
- **Safety:** Contains breath-holds; keep them short. Drop holds if pregnant or dizzy. Not near water/driving.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (risk of dizziness)
  - Never in or near water (breath-hold drowning risk)
  - Glaucoma or raised intraocular pressure: holds can transiently raise IOP — minimize or omit
  - Epilepsy/seizure disorders: blood-gas changes from holds can lower seizure threshold
  - Fainting/vasovagal history or orthostatic intolerance: holds can cause lightheadedness/fainting
  - Pregnancy and uncontrolled hypertension/cardiovascular disease: keep holds minimal or use the no-hold equal variant
  - Panic disorder / high anxiety sensitivity: holds can trigger panic
  - Children: supervised, no-hold equal variant; stop and breathe normally if dizzy or tingling
- *Sources:* Yogic tradition (Sama Vritti); U.S. Navy / tactical breathing (box-breathing variant); Stress-management literature

### Triangle Breathing  `CAUTION`
*aka Three-Part Equal Breathing, Equal Triangle Breath, 3-Sided Breath*

- **Pattern:** in 4.0s / hold 4.0s / out 4.0s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 180s, 6 rounds
- **Route:** Nasal breathing preferred for both inhale and exhale.
- A simple equal-count breath with three phases — inhale, hold, exhale — visualized as the three sides of a triangle. A gentle everyday reset for focus and calm.
- *How it works:* Equal-length phases slow the respiratory rate toward ~5 breaths/min, increasing parasympathetic (vagal) tone and steadying HRV. The short post-inhale hold mildly raises CO2 tolerance without strain and helps interrupt rapid shallow breathing. The hold is genuine breath retention.
- **Benefits:** Quickly steadies attention and focus; Lowers physiological arousal and stress; Easy to remember via the triangle shape; Usable discreetly anywhere
- **Safety:** Contains a short hold; never force it. Drop the hold if pregnant or dizzy. Not while driving.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Pregnancy: keep the hold short or skip it
  - Uncontrolled hypertension / cardiovascular disease: minimize holds
  - Glaucoma or retinal conditions: raised intraocular pressure on breath-hold
  - COPD / respiratory conditions / CO2 retention: holds may worsen air-hunger
  - Epilepsy or seizure disorder
  - Not while driving or operating machinery; not in or near water
  - Eating disorders / panic / dissociation history
  - Children: supervise, keep counts short; if the hold causes breathlessness or anxiety, drop it
- *Sources:* Yogic tradition; General mindfulness/clinical breathing practice

### Viloma (Interrupted / Stepped Breathing)  `CAUTION`
*aka Viloma Pranayama, Interrupted Breathing, Stepped Breathing, Segmented Breath, Ladder Breathing*

- **Pattern:** in 6.0s / hold 2.0s / out 6.0s / hold 0.0s (INTERMEDIATE)
- **Suggested:** 240s
- **Route:** Nasal breathing with short pauses segmenting the inhale and/or exhale.
- A breath divided into several short pauses on the way in, on the way out, or both, like climbing stairs, with brief holds segmenting a single inhalation or exhalation. Used to slow breathing, build control, and calm the nervous system.
- *How it works:* Pausing repeatedly during the inhale or exhale lengthens the overall breath, builds fine diaphragm control, and adds many short retentions. Segmenting the exhale promotes parasympathetic calming; segmenting the inhale is gently energizing. The brief pauses are short kumbhaka. (Stepped exhale: avoid with low BP; stepped inhale: avoid with high BP.)
- **Benefits:** Builds breath control and lung capacity; Improves focus through stepped counting; Can be calming (stepped exhale) or gently energizing (stepped inhale); Useful preparation for longer breath work
- **Safety:** Multiple short holds; keep pauses easy, never strain. Sit; not near water or driving. Stop if dizzy.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Never in or near water / while swimming or bathing (breath-hold drowning risk)
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (stepped counting plus holds reduce attention)
  - Pregnancy: keep pauses minimal
  - Uncontrolled hypertension or cardiovascular disease (and low BP for the stepped-exhale variant)
  - Glaucoma: breath holds raise intraocular pressure
  - Epilepsy/seizure disorder; panic disorder or anxiety
  - Recent abdominal, eye, or chest surgery, and hernia
  - Respiratory conditions in an acute state (asthma, COPD)
  - Children: very short comfortable pauses under direct supervision; stop and breathe normally if dizzy or air-hungry
- *Sources:* Yogic tradition; Iyengar yoga pranayama pedagogy (Light on Pranayama)

## EVERYDAY

### Five-Finger (Star) Breathing  `GENERAL`
*aka Take Five Breathing, Finger Breathing, Star Breathing, Hand Tracing Breath, Starfish Breathing*

- **Pattern:** in 4.0s / hold 0.0s / out 5.0s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 120s, 5 rounds
- **Route:** Nasal breathing preferred; mouth exhale is fine for children.
- Trace up and down each finger of one hand with the other index finger — inhale tracing up, exhale tracing down. A tactile, grounding paced-breathing tool, great for kids and in-the-moment anxiety.
- *How it works:* Pairing slow breaths with tactile/visual hand-tracing adds multisensory grounding that anchors attention and slows the breath, lowering respiratory rate and engaging the parasympathetic system while distracting from anxious thoughts.
- **Benefits:** Calms anxiety in the moment; Excellent for children and grounding during distress; Combines breathing with tactile focus; No counting needed — the hand sets the pace
- **Safety:** Safe and gentle. Not while driving. Stop and breathe normally if dizzy.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (tracing pulls eyes and attention away — flagged by Cleveland Clinic)
  - Not in or near water due to possible lightheadedness
  - Slow the pace if 4-4 feels like air hunger (esp. children, asthma, panic history, pregnancy)
  - Young children: adult supervision; never force breath length
- *Sources:* Pediatric/school mindfulness programs; Cleveland Clinic / children's anxiety resources

## RELAXATION

### Diaphragmatic (Belly) Breathing  `GENERAL`
*aka Belly Breathing, Abdominal Breathing, Deep Breathing, Adham Pranayama*

- **Pattern:** FREEFORM (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Nasal breathing preferred.
- Breathing by engaging the diaphragm so the belly rises on the inhale and falls on the exhale, with minimal chest movement. The foundational skill underlying most relaxation breathing.
- *How it works:* Diaphragmatic descent draws air into the lower lungs and stimulates vagal afferents, improving breathing efficiency and reducing the work of breathing. It counters shallow stress-breathing and shifts toward parasympathetic activation.
- **Benefits:** Reduces stress and anxiety; Improves breathing efficiency and reduces work of breathing; Foundation for other slow-breathing techniques; Can modestly lower heart rate and blood pressure
- **Safety:** Safe for most. Don't over-deepen. Stop if dizzy or tingling.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (transient lightheadedness)
  - Not in or near water due to fainting risk
  - Rise slowly afterward — slow deep breathing can cause a transient drop in blood pressure
  - Late pregnancy: avoid flat-supine learning position (aortocaval compression); sit or side-lie
  - Severe COPD with hyperinflation: forced abdominal breathing may worsen breathlessness — use under pulmonary-rehab guidance
  - Stop if lightheaded or tingling (sign of over-breathing); don't over-deepen
- *Sources:* American Lung Association; Cleveland Clinic; Pulmonary rehabilitation guidelines

### Dirga (Three-Part Yogic Breath)  `GENERAL`
*aka Three-Part Breath, Complete Yogic Breath, Dirgha Pranayama, Deep Yogic Breathing*

- **Pattern:** in 5.0s / hold 0.0s / out 6.0s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Nasal breathing throughout; slow and deep.
- A foundational deep-breathing practice that fills the breath in three parts (belly, lower ribs, upper chest) and releases in reverse. Teaches full diaphragmatic breathing and is deeply grounding.
- *How it works:* Sequentially expanding belly, ribcage, and chest recruits the full breathing apparatus, especially the diaphragm. The slow, exhale-biased pace (~5.5 breaths/min) strongly activates the parasympathetic nervous system, lowering heart rate and stress. No retention.
- **Benefits:** Teaches full, efficient diaphragmatic breathing; Calms the nervous system and reduces stress; Improves breath awareness; Grounding foundation before other pranayama
- **Safety:** Very safe with no holds. Sit or lie down; stop if dizzy.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (grounding/relaxation can reduce alertness)
  - Practice seated or lying down until comfortable (transient lightheadedness on standing)
  - Not in or near water
  - Significant cardiovascular condition: check with a clinician before regular practice
  - Keep hold values at zero — do not add breath-holds (this keeps it pregnancy-appropriate and safe)
- *Sources:* Yogic tradition; Modern yoga therapy and breath-training pedagogy

### Paced Slow Breathing (~6 breaths/min)  `GENERAL`
*aka Slow Breathing, Paced Respiration, Controlled Breathing, 6 BPM Breathing*

- **Pattern:** in 4.0s / hold 0.0s / out 6.0s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Nasal breathing preferred; smooth and unforced.
- Deliberately slowing breathing to roughly 5-7 breaths per minute (about 4s in / 6s out) to reduce anxiety, lower blood pressure, and raise heart-rate variability. A first-line, low-risk self-regulation tool.
- *How it works:* Slowing respiration toward ~6 breaths/min synchronizes breathing with baroreflex and heart-rate rhythms, maximizing vagal tone and HRV. The extended exhale activates the parasympathetic brake, lowering heart rate, blood pressure, and sympathetic arousal.
- **Benefits:** Reduces anxiety, acute stress, and sympathetic arousal; Lowers blood pressure (adjunct in hypertension); Increases heart-rate variability and vagal tone; Easy, portable, and safe for daily use
- **Safety:** One of the safest techniques. Keep breaths gentle. Stop if dizzy; rise slowly.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (possible transient lightheadedness)
  - Not in or near water / while submerged
  - Very low resting blood pressure or orthostatic hypotension: practice seated and rise slowly
  - On antihypertensive medication: watch for additive blood-pressure lowering
  - If slowing causes air hunger, increase the rate slightly
  - Not a replacement for prescribed medication
- *Sources:* Russo et al. (physiology of slow breathing); American Heart Association (device-guided breathing for BP); ACSM / general clinical guidance

### Coherent (Resonance) Breathing  `GENERAL`
*aka Resonance Frequency Breathing, Resonant Breathing, 5.5 Breathing, Cardiac Coherence, Six-Breaths-Per-Minute Breathing, HRV Breathing*

- **Pattern:** in 5.5s / hold 0.0s / out 5.5s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 600s
- **Route:** Smooth, quiet nasal breathing in and out; no pause between phases.
- Smooth, equal breathing at about 5-6 breaths per minute (roughly 5.5s in, 5.5s out, no holds) to maximize heart-rate variability and autonomic balance. A sustainable everyday calm-and-focus reset.
- *How it works:* Around 5-6 breaths/min the cardiovascular system reaches its resonance frequency (~0.1 Hz), where respiration, heart rate, and the baroreflex synchronize and HRV peaks. No holds, so it is gentle and sustainable. Individual resonance varies (~4.5-6.5/min); 5.5 is a good default.
- **Benefits:** Maximizes heart-rate variability (HRV); Steadies mood and supports calm focus; May lower blood pressure with regular practice; Sustainable for long, daily sessions
- **Safety:** Very safe; no holds. Stop if dizzy or tingling.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (can reduce alertness / cause lightheadedness)
  - Not in or near water
  - Low blood pressure / orthostatic hypotension or fainting-prone: lowers BP — sit and rise slowly
  - Pregnancy: generally fine, but keep the pace comfortable and never strain
  - Significant cardiovascular or respiratory condition: check with a clinician before regular practice
  - Stop if dizzy, lightheaded, tingling, or chest-tight
- *Sources:* Lehrer & Gevirtz (resonance frequency / HRV biofeedback); Stephen Elliott (Coherent Breathing); Brown & Gerbarg (Breath-Body-Mind)

### Equal Breathing (no holds)  `GENERAL`
*aka Sama Vritti (no-hold), Even Breathing, Same-Length Breathing, 4-4 Equal Breath*

- **Pattern:** in 5.0s / hold 0.0s / out 5.0s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Nasal breathing preferred for both phases.
- Inhaling and exhaling for the same count (commonly 5/5, or 4/4, 6/6) with no breath holds — a simpler cousin of box breathing without retentions. A gentle entry point to paced breathing.
- *How it works:* Matching inhale and exhale length at a slowed rate (near 6 breaths/min at 5/5) steadies the autonomic nervous system, supports vagal tone, and creates a calming rhythm. Without holds it suits people who find retention uncomfortable.
- **Benefits:** Calms and steadies the nervous system; Easy for beginners and before sleep; Improves focus through rhythmic counting; No retention — comfortable for most
- **Safety:** Very safe. Pick a count you can sustain (4/4, 5/5, 6/6). Stop if dizzy.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Not while driving or operating machinery
  - Not in or near water due to lightheadedness risk
  - Sit or lie down and rise slowly (slowed breathing can cause dizziness in some)
  - Anxiety/panic-prone: interoceptive focus may trigger paradoxical anxiety — stop and breathe normally
  - Pregnancy / cardio-respiratory conditions: keep the pace comfortable, no strain
  - Children: shorter comfortable count and supervision
- *Sources:* Yogic pranayama tradition (Sama Vritti); General clinical relaxation guidance

### Extended-Exhale Breathing (1:1.5 ratio)  `GENERAL`
*aka Prolonged Exhalation, 4-6 Breathing, Long-Exhale Breathing, Relaxing Breath (exhale-emphasis)*

- **Pattern:** in 4.0s / hold 0.0s / out 6.0s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Inhale through the nose; exhale through nose or pursed lips.
- Making the exhale longer than the inhale (e.g. inhale 4, exhale 6) without breath holds. A direct, reliable way to down-regulate the nervous system.
- *How it works:* The heart slows on exhalation (respiratory sinus arrhythmia, via the vagus nerve). Lengthening the exhale relative to the inhale maximizes time in the vagally-dominant, heart-slowing phase, increasing parasympathetic tone and promoting calm.
- **Benefits:** Rapidly lowers heart rate and arousal; Reduces anxiety and stress; Easy to titrate (adjust exhale length to comfort); No retention — gentle and accessible
- **Safety:** Very safe. Keep the exhale smooth and unforced. Stop if dizzy.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (slow breathing can cause lightheadedness)
  - Not in or near water / while bathing
  - Low blood pressure / fainting tendency: lowers HR and BP — practice seated and rise slowly
  - If the long exhale causes breathlessness, shorten it (e.g. 4-in/5-out)
  - Stop if dizzy, lightheaded, or short of breath
- *Sources:* Paced-breathing / RSA literature; Yogic pranayama (exhale-emphasis); Huberman Lab / Stanford (exhale-emphasis)

### 1:2 Calming Reset (Double-Length Exhale)  `GENERAL`
*aka Double-Exhale Breath, 1:2 Ratio Breathing, 4-8 Breathing*

- **Pattern:** in 4.0s / hold 0.0s / out 8.0s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 180s
- **Route:** Inhale through the nose; exhale slowly through nose or gently pursed lips.
- Make the exhale about twice as long as the inhale (e.g. inhale 4, exhale 8) to down-shift the nervous system quickly. A go-to everyday calming reset, no holds.
- *How it works:* A longer exhale relative to the inhale amplifies parasympathetic (vagal) activity: the vagus nerve slows the heart during exhalation, so emphasizing the out-breath lowers heart rate and arousal. One of the most reliable acute ways to trigger relaxation.
- **Benefits:** Rapidly reduces stress and anxiety; Lowers heart rate and arousal; Useful before sleep or to defuse tension; Simple ratio, easy to scale (4:8, 5:10)
- **Safety:** Keep the exhale relaxed, not forced. Sit down; stop if lightheaded.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (transient lightheadedness)
  - Not in or near water / while bathing or swimming
  - If extending the exhale causes air hunger, shorten it; do not strain to lengthen
  - Significant cardiovascular or respiratory condition: clinician clearance before regular practice
  - Practice seated until you know how you respond; stop if faint or dizzy
- *Sources:* Autonomic / vagal-tone research; Cyclic-sighing / exhale-emphasis research (Balban et al., Stanford 2023); Yogic and clinical breathing practice

### Physiological Sigh  `GENERAL`
*aka Double Inhale, Sigh Breathing, Double-Breath, Double-Inhale Reset*

- **Pattern:** in 2.0s / hold 0.0s / out 6.0s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 300s, 5 rounds
- **Route:** Double inhale through the nose; long exhale through the mouth.
- A double inhale through the nose (a full breath in, then a second short top-up sniff) followed by a long, slow exhale through the mouth. The fastest known breathing tool to reduce acute stress in real time.
- *How it works:* The second sharp inhale re-inflates collapsed alveoli, maximizing surface area for offloading CO2. The long exhale slows the heart via the vagus nerve and discharges CO2, rapidly reducing arousal. Done repeatedly (cyclic sighing) it improves mood over time.
- **Benefits:** Fastest real-time relief from acute stress/anxiety; Lowers heart rate within a breath or two; Relieves the sensation of breathlessness; Works in a single breath, anywhere, no equipment
- **Safety:** Safe for nearly everyone. Two inhales, one long exhale. Ease off if lightheaded.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (rare lightheadedness from repeated deep inhales)
  - Avoid repeated rounds in or near water due to faint risk
  - If prone to dizziness, do it seated or lying down
  - Seek medical care if breathlessness is severe, sudden, or cardiac in nature rather than stress-driven
  - Ease off if rapid deep breaths cause lightheadedness
- *Sources:* Huberman & Spiegel, Stanford (Cell Reports Medicine 2023); Feldman/Krasnow respiratory neuroscience (sigh circuitry)

### Cyclic Sighing (5-minute protocol)  `GENERAL`
*aka Repeated Physiological Sighs, Sigh-Based Cyclic Breathing*

- **Pattern:** in 2.0s / hold 0.0s / out 6.0s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Double inhale through the nose; long exhale through the mouth.
- A structured 5-minute practice of repeated physiological sighs (double inhale, long exhale) shown in a controlled trial to improve mood and lower stress over a daily 5-minute dose. No retention.
- *How it works:* Each cycle uses a double inhale to fully inflate the lungs and offload CO2, then a prolonged exhale that engages the vagus nerve to slow the heart. Sustained over five minutes daily, this exhale-emphasized pattern lowers resting respiratory rate and improves positive affect.
- **Benefits:** Improves daily mood / positive affect; Reduces stress and anxiety; Lowers resting respiratory rate over time; Short, structured, evidence-based daily protocol
- **Safety:** Safe for nearly everyone. Repeated double-inhale/long-exhale; keep it relaxed.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Stop if you feel dizzy, lightheaded, or short of breath (the double inhale can cause transient lightheadedness)
  - Not studied in moderate-to-severe psychiatric conditions — those individuals should consult a clinician
  - Pregnancy: keep it gentle, do not force the inhale
  - Get comfortable seated before doing it while driving or operating machinery
- *Sources:* Balban, Huberman & Spiegel, Stanford (Cell Reports Medicine, 2023)

### Chandra Bhedana / Left-Nostril Calming Breath  `GENERAL`
*aka Chandra Bhedana, Chandra Bhedi, Left Nostril Breathing, Moon-Piercing Breath, Lunar Breath, Chandra Anuloma Viloma (left-only variant)*

- **Pattern:** in 4.0s / hold 0.0s / out 6.0s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Nasal only — left nostril, right nostril closed with the thumb/finger.
- A cooling, calming nostril breath: inhale through the left nostril (or breathe slowly only through the left), traditionally said to stimulate the soothing lunar (ida) channel. Used to relax and prepare for sleep.
- *How it works:* Left-nostril breathing is associated with greater parasympathetic activity, a cooling/calming quality, and reduced arousal. The slow single-nostril pace also lowers respiratory rate and actively lowers heart rate and blood pressure, which contributes to the calming effect.
- **Benefits:** Promotes relaxation and calm before sleep; May lower heart rate and blood pressure; Reported cooling/soothing effect; Simple focusing practice
- **Safety:** Calming, no holds. Caution if low BP. Not while driving; stop if dizzy.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Low blood pressure / hypotension: actively lowers BP and HR — can cause dizziness or fainting
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (sedating/sleep-prep)
  - Not in or near water
  - Stop if you feel dizzy, lightheaded, or faint
  - Avoid if a nostril is fully blocked (cold/congestion)
  - Pregnancy: practice gently and consult a clinician
  - Uncontrolled/unstable cardiovascular conditions: consult a clinician
  - If a breath-hold (kumbhaka) is added, treat as CAUTION and apply hold-related cautions
- *Sources:* Yogic pranayama tradition (Chandra Bhedana); Hatha Yoga Pradipika; Nasal-cycle / autonomic research (Telles et al.)

### Nadi Shodhana (Alternate-Nostril Breathing)  `GENERAL`
*aka Nadi Shodhana, Anuloma Viloma, Nadi Shuddhi, Channel-Cleaning Breath, Alternate Nostril Breathing*

- **Pattern:** in 4.0s / hold 0.0s / out 6.0s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Nasal only — alternating nostrils, closed with thumb and ring finger.
- A balancing pranayama where you breathe alternately through one nostril at a time — inhale left, exhale right, inhale right, exhale left — using the fingers to close each nostril. One of the most widely taught, best-tolerated breathing practices. No retention in this version.
- *How it works:* The slow alternating pattern lengthens the breath cycle and extends the exhale, engaging the parasympathetic system, increasing vagal tone and HRV. RCT/meta-analysis evidence supports modest systolic BP reduction. The manual rhythm also anchors focus.
- **Benefits:** Calms the nervous system and reduces stress; Improves heart-rate variability and vagal tone; Improves focus and a sense of balance; Good pre-meditation or pre-sleep practice
- **Safety:** Safe without holds. Sit stably, not while driving. Stop if dizzy.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Not while driving or operating machinery — sit in a stable position (slow breathing can cause lightheadedness even without holds)
  - Stop and return to normal breathing if dizzy, lightheaded, faint, or nauseous
  - Avoid if nostrils are congested/blocked, or with recent nasal/sinus surgery, sinus infection, fever, or acute respiratory illness
  - Asthma, COPD, or chronic lung disease: consult a clinician before the basic version too
  - Practice on a relatively empty stomach (not right after a large meal)
  - Children: supervise
  - If breath retention is added it becomes CAUTION — apply hold cautions (pregnancy, hypertension, cardiovascular disease, panic)
- *Sources:* Yogic pranayama tradition (Nadi Shodhana); Telles et al. and yoga-physiology studies; Brown & Gerbarg

### Bhramari (Humming Bee Breath)  `GENERAL`
*aka Bhramari, Bee Breath, Humming Breath, Brahmari*

- **Pattern:** in 4.0s / hold 0.0s / out 8.0s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Inhale and exhale through the nose; exhale carries the humming sound.
- A slow nasal inhale followed by a long exhale while making a steady humming/bee-like sound (often with the ears gently closed). The vibration and extended exhale are deeply soothing and quiet the mind.
- *How it works:* Humming greatly lengthens the exhale (boosting vagal, heart-slowing tone), produces head/sinus vibration, and raises nasal nitric oxide. The auditory and vibratory focus calms the nervous system and reduces mental chatter. The extended exhale is the main parasympathetic driver.
- **Benefits:** Calms the mind and reduces anxiety/agitation; Lengthens exhale and boosts vagal tone; May aid sleep and ease tension headaches; Increases nasal nitric oxide (a biomarker finding, not a proven clinical cure)
- **Safety:** Gentle hum, no retention. Skip if epilepsy/heart/BP concerns; not while driving.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Epilepsy / seizure disorders: breath modulation plus sustained vibration is a commonly listed caution
  - Severe or uncontrolled hypertension: keep humming gentle/short, never with retention
  - Cardiovascular disease, recent cardiac event, or chest pain: gentle and short only, no retention
  - Pregnancy: keep gentle, avoid any retention, consult a clinician
  - Glaucoma or eye conditions: never apply eye pressure; skip the eye-blocking gesture
  - Active ear infection, recent ear surgery, or perforated eardrum: do not close/press the ears
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (eyes-closed, meditative); practice seated, not supine; stop if dizzy
- *Sources:* Yogic pranayama tradition (Bhramari); Nasal nitric oxide research (Weitzberg & Lundberg); Yoga-physiology studies

### Ujjayi (Ocean / Victorious Breath)  `CAUTION`
*aka Victorious Breath, Ocean Breath, Ujjayi Pranayama, Psychic Breath, Darth Vader Breath*

- **Pattern:** in 5.0s / hold 0.0s / out 6.0s / hold 0.0s (INTERMEDIATE)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Nasal breathing on both phases, with gentle throat (glottal) constriction.
- Slow nasal breathing with a gentle constriction at the back of the throat (glottis), creating a soft ocean-like sound on inhale and exhale. Used to steady and calm the breath, including during flowing yoga. No retention in this version.
- *How it works:* The slight glottal narrowing slows airflow and lengthens both phases, reducing respiratory rate and raising vagal tone. The sound provides a feedback anchor. The throat resistance is a mild Valsalva-like maneuver that can transiently raise blood pressure during the breath — the basis for the cardiovascular and pregnancy cautions.
- **Benefits:** Slows and smooths the breath; calming; Improves focus and breath awareness; Lowers respiratory rate and arousal; Helps regulate effort during movement or yoga
- **Safety:** Keep the throat sound soft, never forced. Caution with heart/BP/pregnancy. Stop if dizzy.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Cardiovascular disease / recent cardiac event: the glottal-resistance pressor effect can transiently raise BP — practice only gently or under supervision
  - Uncontrolled hypertension: keep the constriction very gentle or avoid unsupervised
  - Hypotension: can lower BP/HR — stop if lightheaded
  - Pregnancy (esp. 2nd/3rd trimester): very gentle version only, no abdominal bracing, with provider clearance
  - Active respiratory irritation, asthma/COPD flare, or throat/laryngeal infection: can provoke coughing/bronchospasm — stop if it triggers coughing or wheeze
  - Not while driving or operating machinery; not in or near water
  - Don't over-constrict the throat; stop if dizzy or it causes throat irritation
- *Sources:* Yogic pranayama tradition (Ujjayi); Modern Hatha/Vinyasa yoga pedagogy; Yoga-physiology literature

### Sitali (Cooling Breath)  `GENERAL`

### Box Breathing  `CAUTION`
*aka Square Breathing, Four-Square Breathing, 4-4-4-4 Breathing, Sama Vritti (equal, related)*

- **Pattern:** in 4.0s / hold 4.0s / out 4.0s / hold 4.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Nasal breathing preferred for both inhale and exhale.
- Equal four-phase breath — inhale, hold, exhale, hold — each for the same count (classically 4 seconds), forming a 'box'. Widely used by Navy SEALs and first responders to stay calm and focused under stress.
- *How it works:* The slow, equal cadence (16s cycle = ~3.75 breaths/min at 4-4-4-4) and the brief post-inhale and post-exhale holds increase parasympathetic (vagal) tone and baroreflex sensitivity. The mild holds gently raise CO2 and train CO2 tolerance, while structured counting occupies attention and interrupts rumination.
- **Benefits:** Rapid calming under acute stress; Improves focus and composure under pressure; May lower heart rate and steady HRV; Provides a structured, controllable rhythm; Easy to learn and remember
- **Safety:** Holds are brief; if dizzy, drop them and breathe slowly. Not while driving or near water.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (breath holds can cause lightheadedness)
  - Not in or near water / while swimming (any breath-hold carries blackout risk)
  - Epilepsy or seizure disorder: CO2 shifts can lower seizure threshold — consult a clinician
  - Pregnancy and uncontrolled hypertension / cardiovascular disease: keep holds short and gentle
  - Severe respiratory disease (COPD/asthma): holds and CO2 rise may worsen air-hunger — keep holds minimal or use a no-hold paced pattern
  - Panic disorder: holds can trigger air-hunger — shorten or skip them
  - Eating disorders / dissociation history: allow opting out of holds
  - Children: shortened holds and supervision; if dizzy, drop the holds
- *Sources:* U.S. Navy SEALs / Mark Divine (tactical breathing); Huberman Lab / Stanford; Cleveland Clinic

### Pursed-Lip Breathing  `GENERAL`
*aka PLB, Pursed-Lips Breathing, Whistle Breathing, Candle Breath (gentle)*

- **Pattern:** in 2.0s / hold 0.0s / out 4.0s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Inhale through the nose; exhale through pursed lips.
- Inhale gently through the nose (~2 counts), then exhale slowly through lips pursed as if blowing out a candle (~4 counts). A cornerstone rescue/as-needed technique for shortness of breath in COPD and asthma.
- *How it works:* Pursing the lips creates back-pressure that keeps airways open longer during exhalation, preventing small-airway collapse and air-trapping. This relieves breathlessness, slows the respiratory rate, and engages the parasympathetic system. Overuse can wash out too much CO2.
- **Benefits:** Relieves shortness of breath in COPD and emphysema; Reduces air-trapping / hyperinflation; Slows respiratory rate and increases efficiency; Portable self-management tool during exertion or panic-breathlessness
- **Safety:** A rescue tool used as-needed, not 5 min forced. Keep exhale relaxed; stop if dizzy.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Stop and rest if it causes lightheadedness, dizziness, or tingling (CO2 washout) — do not push to 5 continuous minutes of forced exhalation
  - Increasing or involuntary reliance can signal worsening respiratory distress / impending failure — seek urgent care, don't just keep breathing
  - Interstitial lung disease (ILD/pulmonary fibrosis): may reduce exercise tolerance — use only under clinician guidance
  - Keep the exhale relaxed and unforced (forced expiration can worsen air-trapping in COPD)
  - Children: guided by a clinician/respiratory therapist
  - Pregnancy: gentle paced breathing fine; avoid forced exhalation
  - Not a substitute for inhalers, oxygen, or emergency care
- *Sources:* American Lung Association; COPD Foundation; Pulmonary rehabilitation guidelines; StatPearls (NIH)

### Resonance-Frequency / HRV Biofeedback Breathing  `CAUTION`
*aka Resonant Breathing, HRV Biofeedback Breathing, Cardiac Coherence (clinical)*

- **Pattern:** in 5.0s / hold 0.0s / out 5.0s / hold 0.0s (INTERMEDIATE)
- **Suggested:** 600s
- **Route:** Nasal breathing preferred; smooth, low-effort, inhale and exhale roughly equal.
- Breathing at an individual's resonance frequency (about 4.5-6.5 breaths/min, often ~6) to maximize heart-rate variability via biofeedback. Used clinically (as an adjunct) for anxiety, depression, hypertension, asthma, and stress disorders.
- *How it works:* Each person has a resonance frequency where respiration, heart rate, and the baroreflex oscillate in phase, producing maximal HRV amplitude. Breathing near it (~5s in / 5s out) exercises baroreflex gain and vagal tone over repeated sessions. At this cadence untrained people can over-ventilate and become hypocapnic.
- **Benefits:** Adjunct for anxiety and stress; some evidence for depression and PTSD; Improves HRV and autonomic (vagal/baroreflex) regulation; Adjunct for hypertension and asthma symptom control; Builds trainable stress resilience with practice
- **Safety:** Keep breaths shallow and gentle to avoid over-breathing. Sit down; stop if dizzy or tingling.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Stop immediately if dizzy, lightheaded, or tingling/numb in lips or fingers (hypocapnia/respiratory alkalosis is documented at this cadence)
  - Keep breaths shallow and natural — do not deepen the volume
  - Low blood pressure / orthostatic hypotension / fainting history: sit, don't stand; rise slowly
  - Not while driving or operating machinery, or in/near water
  - Pregnancy: clinician-guided, not a substitute for antenatal care
  - Significant cardiovascular/cardiopulmonary disease (arrhythmia, heart failure, uncontrolled hypertension, severe asthma/COPD): begin only with clinician oversight; not a substitute for medication or rescue inhalers
  - Practice seated or supported, not standing, for the first sessions
- *Sources:* Paul Lehrer & Richard Gevirtz (resonance-frequency HRV biofeedback); Association for Applied Psychophysiology and Biofeedback (AAPB); HRV biofeedback meta-analyses

### Buteyko Reduced Breathing  `CAUTION`
*aka Buteyko Method, Reduced-Volume Breathing, Reduced Breathing, Nasal Breathing Retraining, BBT*

- **Pattern:** in 3.0s / hold 0.0s / out 4.0s / hold 3.0s (INTERMEDIATE)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Strictly nasal breathing throughout.
- A method of deliberately breathing less — slow, light, quiet nasal breathing to create a tolerable, gentle 'air hunger' — to correct chronic over-breathing. Includes a short gentle post-exhale pause (a comfortable Control Pause, not a maximal hold).
- *How it works:* Chronic over-breathing lowers arterial CO2, which can worsen asthma and anxiety. Buteyko trains lighter, slower nasal breathing to normalize CO2 and improve CO2 tolerance, relaxing airways and calming the nervous system. The gentle pauses raise CO2 to recalibrate respiratory drive. The displayed pause is gentle, not a max hold.
- **Benefits:** May reduce asthma symptoms and rescue-inhaler use (adjunct, not a replacement for medication); Improves CO2 tolerance and reduces over-breathing; May help dysfunctional breathing, anxiety-related hyperventilation, and snoring; Promotes habitual nasal breathing
- **Safety:** Keep air-hunger mild, never distressing. Continue medications. Not near water/driving; stop if dizzy.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Do not stop or reduce prescribed asthma/other medication without medical supervision; not a substitute for emergency asthma treatment
  - Not while driving or operating machinery; never in or near water (any breath-hold technique)
  - Pregnancy (gentle practice only; avoid strong holds); panic disorder (introduce air-hunger gradually)
  - Uncontrolled hypertension, cardiovascular disease, recent heart attack, or arrhythmia
  - Epilepsy/seizure disorder; Type 1 diabetes; kidney disease (under guidance only)
  - Sickle cell disease, arterial aneurysm, brain tumor, or recent hemorrhagic stroke/thrombosis (absolute contraindications per Buteyko sources); uncontrolled hyperthyroidism
  - Uncontrolled/severe asthma or during an attack (use reliever, not breath holds); recent surgery; children only with qualified supervision; stop if dizzy or faint
- *Sources:* Konstantin Buteyko / Buteyko Method; Patrick McKeown (Oxygen Advantage / Buteyko); Asthma RCTs of Buteyko breathing

### Buteyko Control Pause (Breath-Hold Assessment)  `CAUTION`
*aka Control Pause, CP, Breath-Hold Time Test, Maximum Pause*

- **Pattern:** ROUNDS (INTERMEDIATE)
- **Suggested:** 60s
- **Route:** After a normal nasal exhale, nose pinched and mouth closed; then resume gentle nasal breathing.
- A Buteyko self-assessment: after a normal exhale, pinch the nose and hold (mouth closed) until the first definite urge to breathe, estimating CO2 tolerance. It is a measurement taken to first urge — not a forced or fixed-duration hold (the 10s value is a gentle nominal placeholder).
- *How it works:* The Control Pause estimates tolerance to CO2 and degree of chronic hyperventilation. A longer comfortable pause correlates with lighter breathing. It must be taken only to the first natural urge to breathe — never strained — and the breath afterward should be calm, not a gasp. Used to track progress, not pushed to maximum.
- **Benefits:** Provides a simple progress marker for Buteyko practice; Builds awareness of breathing efficiency and CO2 tolerance; Helps gauge whether breathing is becoming lighter over time
- **Safety:** A gentle measurement to the FIRST urge — never strained. Seated, never near water. Stop if faint.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Never near or in water, while bathing or swimming; not while driving or operating machinery
  - Pregnancy (avoid breath-holds especially in the first trimester); panic disorder (can provoke air-hunger)
  - Cardiovascular disease, recent heart attack, or arrhythmia; uncontrolled hypertension
  - Epilepsy or seizure disorder; glaucoma or weak/detached retina (Valsalva-type IOP rise)
  - Type 1 diabetes; uncontrolled or severe asthma; recent surgery
  - Sickle cell anemia, arterial aneurysm, brain tumor/raised intracranial pressure, kidney disease, uncontrolled hyperthyroidism (Buteyko absolute contraindications)
  - Eating disorders; severe psychiatric conditions (clearance/supervision); children, esp. under 4; sit during the hold and stop if dizzy or faint
- *Sources:* Buteyko Method (Control Pause assessment); Patrick McKeown / Buteyko Clinic International

### Oxygen Advantage / BOLT Breath-Hold Training  `ADVANCED`

### CO2 Tolerance Table  `CAUTION`
*aka CO2 Table, Carbon Dioxide Tolerance Training, Freediving CO2 Table*

- **Pattern:** ROUNDS (INTERMEDIATE)
- **Suggested:** 900s, 8 rounds
- **Route:** Relaxed nasal breathing on the rest intervals; hold on a full or relaxed breath per protocol.
- Freediving drill: repeated static breath-holds of a FIXED duration with progressively SHORTENING rest intervals, so accumulating CO2 trains tolerance to air hunger. Hold time should be scaled to ~50% of your personal maximum, NOT a fixed number.
- *How it works:* Hold time stays constant while recovery breathing shrinks each round (e.g. rest 2:00 down to 0:45). Starting each hold with less recovery, blood CO2 climbs and you train tolerance to the urge to breathe (hypercapnia) without changing max hold length. The hold should be set to roughly 50% of personal max breath-hold; the shortening rest is the key training variable.
- **Benefits:** Improved tolerance to the urge to breathe (CO2 tolerance); Calmer response to air hunger and stress; Foundation skill for freediving; Better breath control under discomfort
- **Safety:** Scale holds to ~50% of your max. Dry and seated, never near water. Stop if faint.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Practice DRY and seated/lying only — NEVER in or near water without trained freedive supervision and a safety buddy (blackout risk); never while driving; do not hyperventilate beforehand
  - Pregnancy; epilepsy or seizure disorder; panic disorder
  - Cardiovascular disease; uncontrolled hypertension; glaucoma; recent surgery
  - Asthma (call out explicitly) and other respiratory conditions (COPD); diabetes
  - History of fainting/syncope/vasovagal episodes; stroke, TIA, or cerebral aneurysm
  - Eating disorders / disordered eating; children and minors
  - Do not combine with O2 tables the same day; do not practice on a full stomach; stop if you feel faint
- *Sources:* Freediving training methodology (AIDA, Molchanovs, PADI Freediver); Apnea coaching literature

  - Asthma, COPD, or other significant respiratory disease; diabetes; bleeding disorders or blood thinners; children/minors; traditionally learned under an experienced teacher
- *Sources:* Tibetan Buddhist yogic tradition; Kozhevnikov et al. 2013 PLOS ONE (g-tummo body temperature study); Benson et al. (early tummo research)

### Holotropic / Conscious-Connected Breathwork  `ADVANCED`
*aka Holotropic Breathwork, Conscious Connected Breathing, Circular Breathing, Rebirthing Breathwork*

- **Pattern:** FREEFORM (ADVANCED)
- **Suggested:** 1800s
- **Route:** Typically mouth breathing, fast and continuous without pauses.
- Sustained, fast, deep, continuous ('connected') breathing with no pauses between inhale and exhale, maintained for an extended period (often 20-60+ min) to induce strong non-ordinary states. Intense; only with trained facilitators, adults only.
- *How it works:* Prolonged voluntary hyperventilation lowers CO2 and raises blood pH, reducing cerebral blood flow and producing intense altered states, emotional release, tetany (cramping from low ionized calcium), tingling, and sometimes carpopedal spasm. Effects are driven by sustained hypocapnia, not extra oxygen. Sessions are long and emotionally intense; recovery time is needed before driving.
- **Benefits:** Strong altered/non-ordinary states of consciousness; Emotional catharsis and release (in some therapeutic settings); Subjective insight experiences
- **Safety:** Intense; facilitators only, adults only. Expect tetany/cramping and altered consciousness. Never near water or driving.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Only with trained facilitators; adults only; NEVER in or near water and NEVER while driving; do not be left alone; allow recovery time before driving/machinery (altered state can persist)
  - Pregnancy; epilepsy or seizure disorder; cardiovascular disease, angina, or recent heart attack; uncontrolled hypertension; aneurysm (personal/family); stroke/TIA or prior brain/neurological condition; recent concussion/TBI
  - Glaucoma or retinal detachment; recent surgery or acute injury; osteoporosis (intense movement/spasm)
  - Severe mental illness, psychosis, bipolar disorder, panic disorder/severe anxiety, or PTSD with flashbacks/dissociation
  - Asthma or other lung/respiratory conditions (bronchospasm); diabetes (esp. uncontrolled); anticoagulants/blood thinners; eating disorders or known electrolyte imbalance (worsens tetany); thyroid disorders/dysautonomia; children/minors
- *Sources:* Stanislav and Christina Grof (Holotropic Breathwork); Rebirthing / conscious-connected breathwork tradition

## SLEEP

### 4-4-8 Breathing  `CAUTION`
*aka 4-4-8 Relaxing Breath, Long-Exhale Hold Breath*

- **Pattern:** in 4.0s / hold 4.0s / out 8.0s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Inhale through the nose; exhale through nose or pursed lips.
- Inhale for 4, hold for 4, exhale slowly for 8. A gentler relative of 4-7-8 with a shorter hold but the same calming long exhale, used for winding down and sleep.
- *How it works:* Combines a short post-inhale hold with an exhale twice the inhale. The extended exhale enhances vagal heart-slowing while the brief hold mildly raises CO2 and steadies the rhythm — promoting parasympathetic dominance and drowsiness, with less lightheadedness than the 7-count hold of 4-7-8.
- **Benefits:** Promotes relaxation and sleep onset; Long exhale lowers heart rate and arousal; Gentler than 4-7-8 for those who find the 7-count hold hard; Simple structured wind-down
- **Safety:** Short hold; drop it if dizzy and just do 4-in/8-out. Sit or lie down, not while driving.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (a SLEEP technique that deliberately induces drowsiness, plus a breath-hold)
  - Not in or near water (bath, pool, hot tub)
  - Epilepsy / seizure history: paced breathing and holds can lower seizure threshold — get medical clearance
  - Glaucoma / elevated intraocular pressure: hold gently without bearing down, or skip the hold
  - Pregnancy, uncontrolled hypertension, cardiovascular/respiratory disease: use a shorter or no hold
  - Eating disorders: breath-control can be a trigger
  - Children: no-hold variant under supervision
  - Panic disorder: holds can provoke air-hunger — reduce the hold; stop if faint, chest pain, or palpitations
- *Sources:* Prof. Hideyuki Negoro / 2024 peer-reviewed study (PMC11229811); Derived from 4-7-8 / yogic exhale-emphasis

### 4-7-8 Breathing (Relaxing Breath)  `CAUTION`
*aka Relaxing Breath, 4-7-8 Technique, Weil Breath*

- **Pattern:** in 4.0s / hold 7.0s / out 8.0s / hold 0.0s (BEGINNER)
- **Suggested:** 76s, 4 rounds
- **Route:** Inhale through the nose; exhale through the mouth with pursed lips.
- Inhale through the nose for 4, hold for 7, exhale slowly through pursed lips for 8 (often with a 'whoosh'). A short, structured wind-down popularized by Dr. Andrew Weil, rooted in yogic pranayama. Limit to 4 cycles for the first month.
- *How it works:* The exhale is twice the inhale and there is a 7-count hold, strongly biasing the autonomic system toward parasympathetic dominance. Extended exhalation increases vagal output; the moderate hold raises CO2, promoting drowsiness. The fixed counts act as a meditative anchor. The hold is genuine retention.
- **Benefits:** Helps initiate sleep and fall back asleep; Calms acute anxiety and stress; Slows heart rate; Useful for managing cravings and impulsive urges
- **Safety:** Start with 4 cycles only. Keep the ratio, not exact seconds. Sit/lie down, never near water or driving.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Never in or near water (bath, pool, swimming) — breath-hold plus lightheadedness carries shallow-water blackout / drowning risk
  - Not while driving or operating machinery (can cause lightheadedness)
  - Epilepsy / seizure disorder: consult a clinician (breath-retention caution)
  - Glaucoma / raised intraocular pressure: breath holding can transiently raise eye pressure
  - Pregnancy, uncontrolled hypertension, significant cardiovascular or respiratory disease: shorten or skip the hold; keep the ratio if needed
  - Asthma/COPD: the 7s hold can provoke air-hunger — get medical advice
  - Eating disorders or trauma/dissociation history: retention-based interoceptive work can be destabilizing
  - Panic disorder: may provoke air-hunger — reduce hold; children: supervised, age-appropriate only
  - Beginners: max 4 cycles per session for the first month; do seated or lying down
- *Sources:* Dr. Andrew Weil (University of Arizona); Yogic pranayama tradition; Cleveland Clinic

## ENERGIZING

### Surya Bhedana (Right-Nostril Energizing Breath)  `CAUTION`
*aka Surya Bhedi, Right Nostril Breathing, Sun-Piercing Breath, Solar Breath*

- **Pattern:** in 4.0s / hold 0.0s / out 6.0s / hold 0.0s (INTERMEDIATE)
- **Suggested:** 180s
- **Route:** Inhale through the right nostril only; exhale through the left, closing the other nostril.
- An energizing pranayama in which you always inhale through the right nostril and exhale through the left, said to stimulate the warming, activating solar (pingala) channel. This is the gentle no-retention beginner variant.
- *How it works:* Right-nostril breathing is associated with greater sympathetic activation, increased alertness, and a mild warming effect; it raises systolic BP and metabolism. Inhaling consistently through the right nostril invokes this activating quality. The classical form adds retention; this version omits it for safety, hence CAUTION rather than ADVANCED.
- **Benefits:** Increases alertness and energy; Mild warming effect; May counter sluggishness or low energy; Improves concentration
- **Safety:** Stimulating — not late in the day. Avoid with high BP, anxiety, heart disease. Seated only; stop if dizzy.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Uncontrolled hypertension or cardiovascular disease (activating / sympathomimetic)
  - Anxiety or panic disorder (stimulating effect may aggravate)
  - Pregnancy (consult a clinician)
  - Epilepsy/seizure disorder
  - Insomnia or before bedtime (stimulating, not calming)
  - Glaucoma / raised intraocular pressure (sympathetic activation can raise IOP)
  - Severe/uncontrolled hyperthyroidism, fever, or acute illness
  - Not in or near water, while driving, or standing; children only with supervision; stop if dizzy, headache, or palpitations
- *Sources:* Yogic tradition (Hatha Yoga Pradipika); Research on nostril dominance and autonomic activity

### Breath of Fire (Agni Pranayama)  `CAUTION`
*aka Agni Pranayama, Kundalini Breath of Fire, Agni Prana*

- **Pattern:** FREEFORM (INTERMEDIATE)
- **Suggested:** 180s
- **Route:** Nasal only — equal rapid inhale and exhale through the nose.
- Kundalini technique of rapid, rhythmic, equal nasal inhalations and exhalations powered by the navel point (~2-3 breaths/sec), faster and lighter than Kapalabhati, sustained for 1-3 minutes. Produces mild hyperventilation.
- *How it works:* Fast (~2-3 breaths/sec) shallow diaphragmatic pumping with equal active inhale and exhale produces mild hyperventilation and strong sympathetic arousal, generating heat and alertness. Closely related to Kapalabhati but with equal inhale/exhale and continuous rhythmic flow. The CO2 washout can cause dizziness, tingling, or syncope.
- **Benefits:** Energizing and warming; Heightened alertness and vitality; Strengthens the diaphragm/core; Mental clarity in Kundalini practice
- **Safety:** Rapid breathing can cause dizziness/tingling; stop if lightheaded. Never near water or driving; seated.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Respiratory/lung conditions: asthma, COPD, recent respiratory infection, and any history of (or risk for) spontaneous pneumothorax (documented CHEST case report)
  - Pregnancy; menstruation (per Kundalini tradition)
  - Epilepsy or seizure disorder; vertigo/dizziness disorders
  - Uncontrolled hypertension, cardiovascular disease; low blood pressure or fainting/syncope history
  - Glaucoma; recent surgery; hernia; GERD / severe acid reflux
  - Panic disorder; eating disorders (over-breathing/lightheadedness can destabilize)
  - Children; empty stomach (do not practice soon after eating)
  - Never in or near water; not while driving; practice seated; slow down or stop if lightheaded or tingling
- *Sources:* Kundalini Yoga tradition (Yogi Bhajan lineage); Pranayama literature; Kundalini Research Institute

### Kapalabhati (Skull-Shining Breath)  `ADVANCED`
*aka Skull Shining Breath, Skull-Polishing Breath, Kapalbhati, Kapalbhati Pranayama*

- **Pattern:** FREEFORM (ADVANCED)
- **Suggested:** 60s, 3 rounds
- **Route:** Forced rapid exhalations through the nose; inhalation is passive and automatic.
- An energizing cleansing practice of rapid, forceful exhalations driven by sharp abdominal contractions, with passive inhalations between them. Traditionally classified as a cleansing kriya as well as a pranayama. Deliberate hyperventilation.
- *How it works:* Forceful, rapid abdominal exhalations strongly engage the diaphragm and abdominal muscles and lower CO2 (voluntary hyperventilation), producing arousal, alertness, and sometimes tingling or lightheadedness. It is stimulating to the sympathetic nervous system and can cause dizziness or fainting if overdone. The representative pace is ~1-2 breaths/sec.
- **Benefits:** Energizing and invigorating; Increases alertness and clears mental fog; Strengthens and tones abdominal muscles; Traditionally used to clear the respiratory passages
- **Safety:** Hyperventilation-style practice. Never near water, driving, or standing. Stop if dizzy.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Never in or near water, while driving, or standing; stop immediately if dizzy, lightheaded, or short of breath
  - Pregnancy; menstruation (commonly cautioned); on a full stomach
  - Epilepsy or seizure disorder; cardiovascular disease; uncontrolled/high hypertension
  - Glaucoma, detached retina, high myopia, or recent eye surgery (raises intraocular/intracranial pressure)
  - Panic disorder or anxiety; eating disorders
  - Recent abdominal, chest, or eye surgery; hernia; acid reflux/GERD or ulcers
  - Spontaneous pneumothorax / lung barotrauma risk; asthma, COPD, bronchitis, or active respiratory infection
  - Diabetes on insulin/glucose-lowering meds (hypoglycemia risk); stroke history, vertigo, or migraine; vascular stents; children/minors; best learned in person
- *Sources:* Yogic tradition (Hatha Yoga Pradipika, Gheranda Samhita); Clinical caution literature on hyperventilation breathing; Spontaneous pneumothorax case report (PMID 15136413)

### Bhastrika (Bellows Breath)  `ADVANCED`
*aka Bellows Breath, Bellows Breathing, Bhastrika Pranayama*

- **Pattern:** FREEFORM (ADVANCED)
- **Suggested:** 60s, 3 rounds
- **Route:** Forceful, active inhalation and exhalation, usually through the nose.
- A powerful energizing practice of rapid, forceful breathing where BOTH inhalation and exhalation are active and vigorous, like a blacksmith's bellows. Among the most stimulating pranayamas. Deliberate hyperventilation.
- *How it works:* Forceful, rapid inhalation and exhalation strongly recruit the diaphragm, abdominal, and chest muscles, constituting deliberate hyperventilation that sharply lowers CO2 and strongly activates the sympathetic nervous system. This produces intense arousal, heat, and alertness, and can readily cause dizziness, tingling, or fainting if overdone.
- **Benefits:** Strongly energizing and warming; Boosts alertness and combats fatigue; Increases respiratory muscle strength; Often used to build energy before retention practices
- **Safety:** Vigorous hyperventilation, more intense than Kapalabhati. Never near water/driving/standing. Stop if dizzy.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Never in or near water, while driving, or standing; stop at the first dizziness, tingling, or breathlessness; start with very few breaths
  - Pregnancy; menstruation (commonly cautioned); on a full stomach
  - Epilepsy or seizure disorder; cardiovascular disease, heart conditions, or arrhythmia; uncontrolled/high hypertension
  - Glaucoma, recent eye surgery, or diabetic retinopathy/retinal fragility; stroke, TIA, or aneurysm history
  - Panic disorder or anxiety; eating disorders, hyperventilation syndrome, dissociation, or trauma/PTSD
  - Recent abdominal, chest, or eye surgery; hernia; acid reflux/GERD or ulcers
  - Severe asthma or COPD (during a flare), recent COVID/respiratory infection; fainting-prone, low blood pressure, vertigo, or anemia; children/minors; learn in person
- *Sources:* Yogic tradition (Hatha Yoga Pradipika, Gheranda Samhita); Clinical caution literature on hyperventilation breathing

### Energizing Pre-Exercise / Stacked Power Breaths  `ADVANCED`
*aka Pump-Up Breathing, Pre-Lift Breathing, Valsalva Bracing (lifting)*

- **Pattern:** FREEFORM (ADVANCED)
- **Suggested:** 60s
- **Route:** Mix of nose and mouth; forceful breaths then a brief braced hold during the effort.
- Several rapid, deep, forceful breaths to acutely raise arousal and 'pump up' before an explosive effort, often paired with a braced breath-hold (Valsalva) during the effort itself. The hold happens during the lift, not as a metronome step.
- *How it works:* A few fast deep breaths transiently raise sympathetic arousal to prime an explosive effort, while a braced breath-hold (Valsalva) during a heavy lift stabilizes the trunk via raised intra-abdominal pressure. Pre-exercise hyperventilation drops cerebral blood-flow velocity and raises blackout risk; the Valsalva sharply spikes blood pressure, so it must be brief. This is freeform — there is no fixed cadence.
- **Benefits:** Acute arousal/alertness before explosive effort; Trunk stabilization during heavy lifts (Valsalva); Psychological readiness / focus cue
- **Safety:** Combines hyperventilation and a Valsalva hold — high risk. Keep the brace brief. Never near water. Stop if dizzy.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Don't take so many fast breaths that you get lightheaded; never near water; not while driving or operating machinery immediately after (syncope risk); stop and sit/lie down if dizzy
  - Uncontrolled or high blood pressure (Valsalva spikes BP); cardiovascular disease; stroke or cerebrovascular disease history
  - Glaucoma, retinal issues, or diabetic retinopathy (Valsalva retinopathy / IOP rise); aneurysm history
  - Epilepsy or seizure disorder (hypocapnia is a known trigger); migraine
  - Pregnancy; recent surgery; history of fainting
  - Asthma / COPD / respiratory conditions; children and adolescents (do not coach Valsalva psych-up)
- *Sources:* Strength and conditioning practice (ACSM, NSCA); Valsalva maneuver physiology

### Cyclic Hyperventilation with Retention  `ADVANCED`
*aka Cyclic Hyperventilation, Power Breathing, Super-Ventilation with Hold, Huberman-style energizing breathing*

- **Pattern:** ROUNDS (ADVANCED)
- **Suggested:** 600s, 4 rounds
- **Route:** Rapid deep breaths (nose or mouth), then a full-exhale breath-hold; route not strict.
- A stimulating protocol of ~25-30 rapid deep breaths (deliberate hyperventilation) then a breath-hold on the empty lung, repeated in rounds. Overlaps with Wim Hof-style breathing. The structure is rounds-of-breaths-then-one-hold (not a hold after every breath).
- *How it works:* Rapid deep breathing lowers CO2 and raises arousal and adrenaline; the post-exhale hold lets CO2 rebuild and suppresses the urge to breathe. The large blood-gas swings drive alertness and a stress response — energizing but with genuine fainting and shallow-water-blackout risk. The alertness comes from the catecholamine surge, not extra oxygen.
- **Benefits:** Strong energizing effect and increased alertness without caffeine; Transient adrenaline and mood boost; Sense of exhilaration and focus; Improved CO2/stress tolerance over time
- **Safety:** Deliberate hyperventilation can cause sudden fainting. NEVER near water or driving. Sit/lie down.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - NEVER in or near water or while swimming/diving (the hold suppresses the urge to breathe — hypoxic/shallow-water blackout, documented drowning deaths); NEVER while driving or standing — always sit or lie down; stop if you feel unwell
  - Pregnancy; epilepsy or seizure disorder; cardiovascular disease or arrhythmia; uncontrolled hypertension; history of stroke, TIA, aneurysm, or fainting
  - Panic disorder, severe anxiety, bipolar disorder, psychosis/schizophrenia, or PTSD with flashbacks/dissociation
  - Glaucoma or detached retina/retinal disease; recent surgery; migraine with aura or cerebrovascular disease
  - Asthma, COPD, or other respiratory disease (bronchospasm — bring inhaler); diabetes; kidney disease; eating disorders
  - Children/minors / under 18; obtain medical clearance for any cardiovascular, respiratory, neurological, or psychiatric condition
- *Sources:* Balban, Huberman & Spiegel, Stanford (Cell Reports Medicine 2023); Wim Hof Method (related); Documented fainting/drowning-risk safety warnings

### Wim Hof Method Breathing  `ADVANCED`
*aka WHM Breathing, Power Breathing, Controlled Hyperventilation with Retention, Iceman breathing*

- **Pattern:** ROUNDS (ADVANCED)
- **Suggested:** 600s, 3 rounds
- **Route:** Deep full breaths (nose or mouth), then a long exhale-hold, then a recovery inhale-hold.
- A modern method (inspired by Tibetan Tummo) of 30-40 deep power-breaths followed by a long breath-hold on the exhale, then a ~15s recovery hold on the inhale, repeated for several rounds. Highly stimulating with serious safety requirements.
- *How it works:* Rapid deep breathing washes out CO2 (hyperventilation) and transiently raises blood oxygen; the long exhale retention is comfortable because CO2 is low. The cycle drives large swings in blood gases, adrenaline, and pH, which is why fainting risk is real and water/standing situations are dangerous. The ~15s recovery inhale-hold follows the long exhale-hold.
- **Benefits:** Strong energizing and alertness boost; Subjective stress resilience and mood lift; May transiently raise adrenaline and modulate the inflammatory response (some studies); Improved breath-hold / CO2 tolerance over time
- **Safety:** Hyperventilation plus long holds — can cause fainting. NEVER near water or driving. Sit/lie down.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - NEVER in or near water (documented drowning deaths), while driving, standing, or in a bath/pool — always sit or lie down in a safe place; can cause fainting without warning; stop if you feel unwell
  - Pregnancy; epilepsy or seizure disorder; cardiovascular disease, history of heart attack or stroke, or arrhythmia; uncontrolled/severe hypertension; history of aneurysm or fainting
  - Panic disorder, severe anxiety, or history of psychosis; migraine with aura
  - Glaucoma, raised intraocular pressure, retinal detachment, or recent eye surgery; recent surgery
  - Severe asthma or COPD during a flare; diabetes (hypoglycemia / altered awareness — official WHM contraindication)
  - Children/minors (a wrongful-death case involved a 17-year-old); obtain medical clearance for any cardiovascular, respiratory, neurological, or psychiatric condition
- *Sources:* Wim Hof Method (WHM); Tibetan Tummo tradition (inspiration); Kox et al. PNAS 2014; Documented drowning-risk safety warnings

## PRANAYAMA

### Kumbhaka (Breath Retention)  `ADVANCED`
*aka Breath Retention, Antara Kumbhaka (after inhale), Bahya Kumbhaka (after exhale), Breath Holding*

- **Pattern:** in 4.0s / hold 8.0s / out 6.0s / hold 4.0s (ADVANCED)
- **Suggested:** 240s
- **Route:** Nasal breathing; breath held with the airway gently closed, not strained.
- The classical practice of deliberately holding the breath, either after a full inhalation (antara) or a full exhalation (bahya). A core, advanced element of traditional pranayama, not an everyday breath. Build up gradually; never hold to strain.
- *How it works:* Voluntary breath retention raises CO2 and trains tolerance to air hunger, influencing chemoreceptor sensitivity and autonomic balance. Retention after exhale and after inhale produce different effects, and the technique meaningfully changes blood gases and pressures (including raising intraocular pressure), which is why it carries real contraindications.
- **Benefits:** Builds CO2 tolerance and breath control; Deepens meditative focus and inner stillness; Traditionally considered the heart of pranayama; May enhance the effects of other breathing practices
- **Safety:** Advanced retention. Never near water/driving/standing. Never strain. Learn from a teacher.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Never practice in or near water, while driving, or standing; never hold to the point of strain or gasping
  - Pregnancy; cardiovascular disease, heart conditions, or arrhythmia; uncontrolled/high hypertension
  - Glaucoma or recent eye surgery (retention raises intraocular pressure); epilepsy/seizure disorder
  - Panic disorder or anxiety; stroke history or increased intracranial pressure; recent surgery
  - Eating disorders / disordered eating; children/minors
  - Asthma, COPD, or other respiratory disease (can provoke bronchospasm); uncontrolled diabetes
  - Do not practice on a full stomach; learn from a qualified teacher
- *Sources:* Yogic tradition (Hatha Yoga Pradipika, Patanjali's Yoga Sutras); Clinical caution literature on breath retention

### Anuloma Viloma (Alternate Nostril with Retention)  `ADVANCED`
*aka Nadi Shodhana with Kumbhaka, Alternate Nostril with Breath Retention, Pranayama with 1:4:2 Ratio*

- **Pattern:** in 4.0s / hold 16.0s / out 8.0s / hold 0.0s (ADVANCED)
- **Suggested:** 300s
- **Route:** Nasal only, alternating nostrils, with breath held after the inhalation.
- The classical, advanced form of alternate-nostril breathing that adds timed breath retention (kumbhaka) after the inhale, in the 1:4:2 ratio. The 16s hold is the final advanced stage — build up gradually from gentler ratios.
- *How it works:* Adding retention after inhalation builds CO2 tolerance, while the long slow cycle deeply engages the parasympathetic system. The extended antara kumbhaka markedly increases intensity and physiological demand (and can spike BP / cause syncope) compared with simple alternate-nostril breathing.
- **Benefits:** Deepens the calming, balancing effects of alternate-nostril breathing; Builds CO2 tolerance and breath control; Strong focus and meditative absorption; Traditionally said to purify the nadis
- **Safety:** Long 16s hold — build up slowly. Never near water/driving/standing. Never strain for air.
- **Avoid / caution:**
  - Never practice breath retention in or near water (bathtub/pool — shallow-water blackout); never while driving or standing
  - Pregnancy; uncontrolled hypertension or cardiovascular disease; hypotension / low blood pressure
  - Glaucoma or recent eye surgery (retention raises intraocular pressure); epilepsy/seizure disorder
  - Panic disorder or anxiety (air hunger can trigger panic); recent surgery
  - Asthma, COPD, or other respiratory conditions (broadly, not only during a flare); hernia or recent abdominal surgery
  - Eating disorders; children/minors; do not practice on a full stomach
  - Build up gradually; never strain for air; best learned from a qualified teacher
- *Sources:* Yogic tradition (Hatha Yoga Pradipika); Classical pranayama ratio teachings
